About

A program manager who holds a PhD

The hardest part of scientific program management is not the tools. It is sitting between senior scientific leadership and a cross-functional team and keeping both sides oriented toward the same outcome. That requires scientific depth to understand what the strategy actually means, operational fluency to convert it into a timeline and a risk register, and communication discipline to ensure every stakeholder has the right information at the right moment.

That is the work I have been doing for eight years. I run operating cadences, own stakeholder briefings, manage risk escalation workflows, and build the SOP infrastructure that makes programs reproducible and audit-ready. I have done this across academic medical centers, international field programs, and infectious disease research environments — in each case building the operational function before using it.

I am not a researcher who decided to try operations. I am a program manager who happens to hold a PhD.

My approach

How I start in a new role

Days 1 to 30
Listen, map, and earn trust

Shadow senior leadership and cross-functional partners to map existing operating rhythms, dashboards, and reporting tools. Audit active programs and risk registers for gaps and quick wins. Identify the first standardization opportunity.

Days 31 to 60
Ship an operating rhythm

Stand up at least one new cadence that improves alignment or decision speed. Begin producing weekly status updates usable directly for stakeholder communication. Deliver a first-version improvement to one SOP or reporting gap.

Days 61 to 90
Demonstrate ownership

Manage at least one risk event or change-control proposal end-to-end. Deliver a program readout to senior leadership. Document one measurable improvement to program speed, clarity, or accountability.
